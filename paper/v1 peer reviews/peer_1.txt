The manuscript addresses an important and timely problem: detecting computer-generated product reviews using a lightweight language model under constrained computational resources. The use of Qwen 2.5-0.5B with Rs-QLoRA is practically relevant, and the reported results on the Kaggle Fake Reviews Dataset are strong. The paper is clearly organized and includes useful reporting of class-wise metrics, a confusion matrix, training curves, and comparisons with several internal baselines.
However, I do not think the manuscript currently provides sufficient evidence to support its strongest claims, especially regarding state-of-the-art performance, real-world fake-review detection, and practical deployment.

Major comments
1. The task formulation needs clarification.
The manuscript repeatedly refers to "fake review detection," but the dataset is described as containing Original Reviews and Computer-Generated reviews. A computer-generated review is not necessarily equivalent to a fake or deceptive review. The authors should revise the terminology throughout the manuscript or provide evidence that the computer-generated reviews are indeed deceptive fake reviews.
2. The evaluation is limited to a single balanced dataset.
The model is evaluated only on the Kaggle Fake Reviews Dataset. This dataset is balanced and may contain generation-specific artifacts. Real-world fake-review detection is usually more imbalanced and heterogeneous. The authors should include external validation on additional datasets or, at minimum, perform cross-category evaluation, such as training on some product categories and testing on held-out categories.
3. The state-of-the-art claim is not sufficiently supported.
The manuscript compares the proposed model with prior studies, but the compared methods appear to use different datasets, splits, preprocessing pipelines, and evaluation protocols. Therefore, the claim that the proposed method achieves state-of-the-art performance should be softened unless the baselines are reproduced under identical experimental conditions.

4. More ablation analysis is needed.
The manuscript attributes performance gains to Rs-QLoRA, high-rank adaptation, and the cosine restart scheduler, but the contribution of each component is not isolated. The authors should include ablations comparing standard QLoRA versus Rs-QLoRA, different LoRA ranks, with and without cosine restarts, and with and without label smoothing.

5. The internal model comparisons require more detail.
The comparisons with ModernBERT-Large, Gemma-3-270M, and Qwen r=16 are useful, but it is unclear whether these baselines received comparable hyperparameter tuning. The surprising result that the higher-rank Qwen configuration trains faster than the lower-rank Qwen baseline also needs explanation and supporting details.

6. Reproducibility details are incomplete.

The manuscript should provide the exact Qwen checkpoint, software versions, quantization configuration, exact software framework and library functions used for the train/validation/test splits, random seed handling, prompt or input formatting, classification-head design, pooling method, and number of trainable parameters. The authors should also clarify whether duplicate or near-duplicate reviews were removed before splitting the dataset.

7. Results should include uncertainty estimates.
The reported metrics appear to come from a single split and a single seed. Given the small differences between the proposed method and several strong baselines, the authors should report mean and standard deviation across multiple random seeds, or confidence intervals for the main metrics.

8. The memory-efficiency claim requires stronger support.
The manuscript states that the method requires less than 4 GB of VRAM, but the measurement procedure is not described. Since training was conducted on a Tesla T4 GPU, the authors should report peak allocated memory, monitoring tools, batch size dependence, and whether the reported value refers to training or inference.


9. Error analysis is too limited.
The discussion of false positives and false negatives is mostly speculative. The authors should provide qualitative examples of misclassified reviews and analyze errors by review length, product category, and model confidence.

Minor comments

Some wording should be made more cautious. For example, phrases such as "clearly demonstrate," "state-of-the-art," and "almost perfect" should be softened unless supported by stronger evidence.
The figures are useful, but the standalone JPEG figure pages at the end appear redundant and should be checked against the journal's formatting requirements.
The literature review should more clearly separate narrow binary classification metrics from broader contextual studies in this domain. For reference, please see "Trust at risk: Detecting misinformation in LLM-generated product reviews and its implications for consumer behavior and platform governance."
